## 1. You Don't Need 20% Down

The biggest myth in homebuying. As a first-time buyer, you can put as little as **3% down** on a conventional loan. On a $350,000 home, that's $10,500 — not $70,000. That changes everything.

**But here's what most people don't know about PMI:**

If you put less than 20% down, you'll pay Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI). But PMI isn't one flat rate — it works in **tiers**:

- **3%–4.99% down**: Highest PMI tier
- **5%–9.99% down**: Lower PMI tier
- **10%–14.99% down**: Even lower PMI tier
- **15%–19.99% down**: Lowest PMI tier
- **20%+ down**: No PMI at all

Here's the key: **if you're between tiers, you're overpaying for nothing.** Putting 7% down gets you the exact same PMI rate as putting 5% down. That extra 2% isn't reducing your PMI — it's just sitting in equity you could have kept in savings. Either hit the next tier or save your cash.

Your interest rate on the loan is typically the same whether you put 5%, 10%, 15%, or 20% down. What changes is the PMI cost. So the smart play is to pick a tier — 3%, 5%, 10%, 15%, or 20% — and don't land in between.

## 2. Credit Scores: What Lenders Actually Look At

Your credit score is not pass/fail. Here's how it breaks down:

- **740+**: Best rates, lowest costs. This is the gold standard.
- **700–739**: Still strong. You'll qualify for competitive programs.
- **680–699**: Good options available. May pay slightly higher rates.
- **620–679**: FHA and some conventional programs still work.
- **580–619**: FHA with 3.5% down. Limited conventional options.
- **Below 580**: FHA with 10% down, or consider credit repair first.

Lenders pull all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) and use the **middle score**. If you're buying with a partner, lenders use the **lower** of your two middle scores.

## 3. Pre-Approval vs. Pre-Qualification

These are not the same thing. One gets your offer accepted. The other gets it ignored.

- **Pre-Qualification**: Quick estimate based on what you tell the lender. No verification. Sellers don't take it seriously.
- **Pre-Approval**: Full application, credit pull, income and asset verification. A real commitment from a lender.

In Austin's market, sellers with multiple offers will skip buyers who only have a pre-qualification letter. Get pre-approved before you start looking at homes.

## 4. How Income Affects What You Qualify For

Your income is the single biggest factor in how much house you can afford. Here's how lenders look at it:

- **Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)**: Lenders compare your total monthly debt payments to your gross monthly income. Most conventional loans cap at **45%–50% DTI**.
- **W-2 employees**: Lenders use your base salary, plus any consistent overtime, bonus, or commission income (typically need a 2-year history to count it).
- **Self-employed**: Lenders average your last 2 years of net income from tax returns. If your income is trending down, they use the lower year.
- **Hourly workers**: Your income is calculated using your average hours over the past 2 years, not just your current schedule.
- **Side income / 1099 work**: Only counts if you have a 2-year history of reporting it on your taxes.

**The gap most buyers don't see**: Your gross income determines your loan amount, but your **net income** determines what you can actually afford month to month. Just because you qualify for $400,000 doesn't mean you should spend $400,000. Budget based on what hits your bank account, not your pre-tax number.

## 5. The Real Cost of Buying a Home

The purchase price is just the beginning. Budget for these:

- **Down payment**: 3%–20% of purchase price
- **Closing costs**: Typically 2%–4% of the loan amount
- **Home inspection**: $400–$600
- **Appraisal fee**: $500–$700
- **Homeowner's insurance**: Varies, but budget $1,500–$3,000/year in Texas
- **Property taxes**: Texas has no state income tax, but property taxes average 1.8%–2.2%
- **HOA fees**: If applicable, $100–$500+/month

A $350,000 home with 5% down typically requires $30,000–$40,000 in total upfront costs (including down payment). Some of this can be covered by seller concessions or assistance programs.

## 6. Seller Concessions: Make the Seller Pay Your Costs

Here's something most first-time buyers don't realize: **the seller can pay for your closing costs, prepaids, and more.** It's called a seller concession, and it's one of the most powerful negotiating tools you have.

When you make an offer on a home, you can ask the seller to contribute a percentage of the purchase price toward your costs. Depending on your loan type and down payment, sellers can typically contribute **3%–6%** of the purchase price. On a $350,000 home, that's $10,500–$21,000 — often enough to cover every dollar of your closing costs and prepaids.

**But seller concessions don't just cover costs — they unlock options.**

Once your closing costs and prepaids are covered, leftover concession dollars can be used to **buy down your interest rate**. There are two ways to do this:

**Temporary Rate Buydown (2-1 Buydown)**

A 2-1 buydown reduces your rate for the first two years of the loan. If your note rate is 6.5%:
- **Year 1**: You pay at 4.5% (2% below your rate)
- **Year 2**: You pay at 5.5% (1% below your rate)
- **Year 3+**: You pay the full 6.5%

The seller funds the difference upfront. You get significant monthly savings during the years when you're most cash-strapped — right after buying. This is especially powerful in a higher-rate market because if rates drop, you can refinance before the buydown expires.

**Permanent Rate Buydown (Discount Points)**

Discount points let you permanently lower your interest rate for the life of the loan. One point = 1% of your loan amount, and it typically reduces your rate by about 0.25%.

On a $332,500 loan (5% down on $350,000):
- **1 point ($3,325)** could drop your rate from 6.5% to ~6.25%
- **2 points ($6,650)** could drop your rate from 6.5% to ~6.0%

The savings are permanent. The question is whether the monthly savings justify the upfront cost — and when the seller is paying for it with concessions, the math changes entirely.

**The bottom line**: In any negotiation, don't just negotiate the price. Negotiate the terms. The right seller concession strategy can save you more than a price reduction ever would.

## 7. Texas-Specific Programs Most Agents Don't Mention

Texas has some of the best first-time buyer programs in the country:

- **My First Texas Home**: Below-market interest rates + up to 5% down payment assistance
- **TDHCA DPA**: Down payment and closing cost assistance as a 0% deferred second lien
- **Texas Mortgage Credit Certificate (MCC)**: Federal tax credit of up to $2,000/year for the life of the loan
- **City of Austin DPA**: Up to $40,000 in assistance for qualifying Austin buyers
- **Habitat for Humanity**: Homeownership programs for qualifying families

These programs have income limits and eligibility requirements. Most buyers don't know they exist because most agents don't know to ask about them.

## 8. 5 Deal-Killing Mistakes to Avoid

I see these kill deals every single week:

1. **Opening new credit cards or taking on new debt** before or during the loan process
2. **Making large deposits** without a paper trail (lenders will ask where it came from)
3. **Changing jobs** during the mortgage process
4. **Co-signing for someone else's loan** — this counts as your debt
5. **Skipping the home inspection** to save $500 (this can cost you $50,000)

The golden rule: Don't change anything about your financial life between pre-approval and closing. No new cars. No furniture on credit. No job changes if you can help it.

## 9. How the Mortgage Process Actually Works

From first call to closing day, here's the timeline:

- **Week 1**: Pre-approval application, credit pull, document collection
- **Weeks 2–6**: House hunting with your agent
- **Day of offer**: Submit offer with pre-approval letter
- **Week 1 after contract**: Home inspection, earnest money deposit
- **Weeks 2–3 after contract**: Appraisal, underwriting review
- **Week 4 after contract**: Clear to close, final walkthrough
- **Closing day**: Sign documents, get your keys

Average timeline in Austin: **30–45 days** from accepted offer to closing. Cash purchases can close in 2–3 weeks.

## 10. What Lenders Need from You

Get these ready before you apply:

- **Last 2 years of W-2s** (or tax returns if self-employed)
- **Last 2 months of pay stubs**
- **Last 2 months of bank statements** (all pages, even blank ones)
- **Photo ID** (driver's license or passport)
- **Rental history** for the past 2 years
- **Explanation letters** for any credit issues, gaps in employment, or large deposits

Self-employed borrowers: You'll also need 2 years of business tax returns and a year-to-date profit & loss statement. Plan for more documentation and a longer review process.

## 11. Understanding Your Monthly Payment

Your mortgage payment is more than just principal and interest:

- **P** — Principal: Pays down your loan balance
- **I** — Interest: What the lender charges you to borrow
- **T** — Taxes: Property taxes escrowed monthly
- **I** — Insurance: Homeowner's insurance escrowed monthly
- **PMI/MIP**: Mortgage insurance if you put less than 20% down

Example on a $350,000 home with 5% down at 6.5%:
- Principal & Interest: ~$2,100/month
- Taxes (1.9%): ~$554/month
- Insurance: ~$200/month
- PMI: ~$150/month
- **Total PITI: ~$3,004/month**

This is why the purchase price alone doesn't tell you what you can afford. The rate, taxes, and insurance matter just as much.

## 12. Why Your Lender Choice Matters

Not all lenders are the same. Here's what to look for:

- **Local knowledge**: A lender who knows Austin's market, appraisers, and title companies
- **Communication**: Someone who answers the phone and explains things in plain English
- **Speed**: Can they close on time? Delays lose deals.
- **Loan options**: Access to conventional, FHA, and Texas assistance programs
- **Transparency**: Upfront about rates, fees, and costs — no surprises at closing

**Broker vs. bank vs. correspondent lender — it matters.**

Most buyers don't know there are different types of lenders. Big banks use their own money and their own rates. Brokers shop your loan to multiple investors. But the best pricing often comes from an **IMA correspondent lender** — a lender that underwrites and funds loans in-house but sells to investors at pre-negotiated rates. This gives you wholesale-level pricing with the speed and control of a direct lender.

That's how we operate at Mortgage Solutions LP. We're not a big bank with overhead baked into your rate, and we're not a broker waiting on a third party to approve your file. We underwrite, fund, and close your loan — and we shop for the best pricing on the back end so you don't have to.

The cheapest rate on a website doesn't help if the lender can't close on time or communicate with your agent. Your lender is the most important person on your homebuying team after your agent.
