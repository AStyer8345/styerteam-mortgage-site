When You Can't Control It, Surrender It

Last Friday I picked up my daughter from school and came home to absolute chaos—three kids, running late, the whole thing. I jumped in my wife's car, ran into Home Depot with two of them, and when I pulled up to the curb I left my key sitting in the cupholder. Completely spaced on it.

Saturday morning at 7am I walked out to go to the gym. My car was gone.

Cameras showed three guys rolling through the neighborhood at 2:47am. They weren't targeting my car specifically—they were just looking for easy wins. They saw the key in the cupholder and took it. That was it.

My whole Saturday turned into police reports, insurance calls, getting a rental car. Nine days later—Monday at 5:15am—I'm looking for my AirPods, pull up Find My, and there's a dot five miles away. I drove there in the dark and my car was just sitting on the street. Everything untouched except a Whataburger bag in the back seat.

Here's What's Weird

I wasn't actually stressed about the car.

Not the whole time it was stolen, not during the insurance nightmare, not even Saturday morning when I realized it was gone. I handled it. Police, insurance, rental, next step. That was the energy.

But the deals that fell out that same week? The unexpected bills? That stuff I was carrying. I was spiraling a little, taking it personally, feeling the weight of it.

The difference hit me: the car was completely outside my control. Once it was gone, there was literally nothing to do but handle the logistics. The deals, the bills, the financial pressure—those felt like my responsibility to fix, my failure to prevent, my problem to solve.

So I carried them.

What the Car Actually Taught Me

Surrender isn't giving up. It's not accepting defeat or waving the white flag on your goals.

Surrender is doing your absolute best with what you actually control and releasing the rest.

I control whether I show up on time. I control the work I do, the relationships I build, the follow-ups I make, the effort I put in. I don't control whether rates move, whether a buyer gets cold feet, whether a deal falls apart through no fault of my own. I don't control unexpected bills or market shifts or timing.

If I'm going to carry all of that, I'll burn out. And my family—the reason I'm carrying it in the first place—that's who suffers most.

The people I know who are actually stable, who sleep at night, who show up strong for their families—they've figured out this split. They work like hell on what's theirs. Then they let go of the rest.

What This Means for You

If you're a borrower carrying financial stress right now, some of that is real and legitimate—you should budget, think carefully, make smart decisions about a mortgage. That's your work to do.

But some of what you're carrying isn't yours. Rates moving. The market not cooperating. Someone else's timeline. Those things happen regardless of how hard you stress about them.

Do your part. Make the calls, get your finances in order, figure out what you actually need in a home. Then bring that to the conversation with someone who can help—not carrying all the doubt, just clear on your next step.

If you're in real estate or mortgage business, this applies even more. You can't control the buyer market right now. You can't control rate movements or appraisals or inspection results. You can control the relationship, the communication, the honesty, the options you present.

Show up, do the work, surrender the results.

Personal Corner

My wife asked me Tuesday why I wasn't freaking out about the car being gone. I told her the truth: because there was nothing to do about it except handle it.

She said, "So why are you freaking out about everything else?"

I didn't have a good answer.

I've spent this week trying to figure out which things are actually mine to fix and which ones I'm just carrying weight on. It's harder than you'd think. There's always this feeling that if I just worry hard enough, think about it enough, plan enough, I can prevent bad outcomes.

I can't. Neither can you.

What we can do is show up. Do the work. Stay honest. Keep moving.

The rest—the car being there nine days later, untouched, still drivable—that's just grace. I didn't earn it by stressing about it. It just happened because I did my part and let go of the rest.
