What You Need to Know About Mortgage Options

Real talk: picking a mortgage can feel like you're drowning in acronyms and numbers. FHA, VA, Conventional, Fixed, ARM — it's a lot. But here's the thing: you don't need to understand everything. You just need to understand what works for your situation.

I'm going to break down the main paths people take when they're buying a home in Austin. Not to overwhelm you. Just to get you clear on what's actually available and what might make sense for you.

The Four Main Mortgage Types

Most people fall into one of these buckets. Which one are you?

  • Conventional loans: You put down at least 3% to 20% (or more). Credit score usually needs to be 620+. No government backing, which sometimes means slightly lower rates. Most common choice for people with decent credit and some savings.
  • FHA loans: Designed for first-time buyers or people with lower down payments. You can put down as little as 3.5%. Credit score can be lower (580+). More flexible on income and debt ratios. The catch: you'll pay mortgage insurance (MIP) for the life of the loan if you put down less than 10%.
  • VA loans: If you served, this is the best deal going. Zero down payment. No mortgage insurance. Rates are usually competitive. If you qualify, honestly, use this.
  • USDA loans: Rural or suburban properties in qualifying areas. Also zero down. Similar benefits to VA loans but different eligibility rules.

The right choice depends on three things: how much you can put down, your credit situation, and whether you qualify for any special programs. Get pre-qualified and we'll figure out which one actually applies to you. No guessing required.

Fixed Rate vs. ARM: The Real Difference

This one's simpler than people think.

Fixed rate: Your interest rate stays the same for the entire loan (15 years, 30 years, whatever you pick). Your monthly payment never changes. You always know what you're paying. Most people choose this because it's predictable and straightforward.

ARM (Adjustable Rate Mortgage): Your rate is fixed for a set period (3, 5, 7, or 10 years), then it adjusts annually based on market rates. Could go up. Could go down. Monthly payment changes. Lower initial rate, but there's risk.

Here's when an ARM actually makes sense: You plan to sell or refinance before the rate adjusts, or you're betting rates will drop. For most people buying in Austin planning to stay put? Fixed rate wins. You sleep better at night.

How Much House Can You Actually Afford?

Banks will tell you a number. That doesn't mean you should use it.

The standard math: your total monthly debt (mortgage, car loans, credit cards, student loans) shouldn't exceed 43% of your gross monthly income. So if you make $5,000 a month, you can carry about $2,150 in total monthly debt. That includes the mortgage.

But here's what matters more: what can you actually afford to live on every month? Factor in property taxes (Austin taxes are real), homeowners insurance, HOA fees if applicable, maintenance, and utilities. Then ask yourself: can I still pay this if I get a smaller bonus? If my spouse loses income? If the AC goes out?

Use our mortgage calculator to get a real number. Then sit with it. Talk to your spouse. Sleep on it. Don't just react to what's possible. React to what's smart.

Down Payment: How Much Do You Really Need?

Conventional: 3% minimum, but 20% gets you better rates and no PMI (private mortgage insurance).

FHA: 3.5% minimum.

VA: 0% if you qualify.

Here's what I see most: people stress about getting to 20% because they think it's required. It's not. If you can put down 5-10% and get approved, that's a win. Yeah, you'll pay PMI. But you'll own a home while you're saving that extra 10%. Build equity. Let your house appreciate. PMI goes away once you hit 20% equity (or once you refinance later and don't need it).

The math almost always works in your favor. Don't wait years to save 20% if you can buy now with 5-10%.
